• mānavaka (sometimes written for Skt. māṇavaka, as LV 101.9 and 108.5, all mss.; f. mānavikānāṃ, perh. rather to Skt. mānava, mānavī, human being, Mv ii.432.4, used of king's wives), m. or nt., in Av i.265.7 is, or corruptly represents, a word meaning peg, post, or the like, for hanging clothes: yena…sā yamalī (q.v.) krītā, tena mānavake sthāpitā Av i.265.7; Tib. cited by Speyer as gdaṅ, which (or gdaṅ bu, Mvy 9037) = carpaṭaka, q.v. The context proves that this is approximately right in mg. But Speyer's suggestion that we em. tenārambhaṇake (should be °mbaṇake, see ārambaṇaka) is improbable; that word seems to mean a different kind of peg, tho also rendered by Tib. gdaṅ (bu). Could our word be related to AMg. māṇavaya, n. of a divine ‘caitya-stambha’ (see Ratnach. s.v.)? [429,2]

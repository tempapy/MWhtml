(s), nirgranthaḥnirgrantha; ‘patron- saint,’ iṣṭadevatā; ‘a company of saints,’ siddhasaṃghaḥsiddhasaṃgha; ‘a suc- cession of saints,’ siddhaparamparā; ‘the seven great saints forming the Great Bear constellation,’ saptarṣayaḥsaptarṣaya m. pl. These are sometimes called Prajapatis, and, as created by Brahma, Brahmarshis or Brahmādikas. Their names are marīciḥmarīci, atriḥatri, aṅgiras, pulastyaḥpulastya or pulastiḥpulasti, pulahaḥpulaha, kratuḥkratu, vaśiṣṭhaḥvaśiṣṭha. Some authorities add the names of bhṛguḥbhṛgu, dakṣaḥdakṣa, kaśyapaḥkaśyapa nāradaḥnārada and pracetas to the list of Brahmarshis or Prajapatis. According to the Vishnu Purāna there are three kinds of Rishis or saints. 1. Brahmarshis, brahmarṣayaḥbrahmarṣaya m. pl., or saints who are the sons of Brahmā, or Brahmans, such as Marīchi, Atri, Vaśiṣtha, &c., dwelling in the sphere of Brahma 2. Devarshis, devarṣayaḥdevarṣaya m. pl., or saints who are also demigods, as Nārada, Nara, Nārāyana, Kardama, &c., dwelling in the region of the gods. 3. Rājarṣis, rājarṣayaḥrājarṣaya m. pl., royal saints or holy personages who are also kings or men of the military class, and who have attained to the rank of Rajarshi from the practice of re- ligious austerities. Such were Ikṣvāku, Viśvamitra, son of Gadhi, Purūravas, Dushmanta, &c. Their sphere is the heaven of Indra. Four other classes of Rishis are enumerated in the Amara Kosha, viz. 1. Maharshis, nahavayaḥnahavaya m. pl., or great saints, such as Vyāsa, the arranger of the Vedas. &c. 2. Paramarshis, paramarṣayaḥparamarṣaya m. pl., or most excellent saints, such as the Muni Bhela, the author of some medical works. 3. Kāndarṣis, kāṇḍarṣayaḥkāṇḍarṣaya m. pl., or saints and sages who teach a particular kānda or section of the Vedas, as Jaimini, founder of the Pūrva Mīmānsa system, &c. 4. Śrutarṣis, śrutarṣayaḥśrutarṣaya m. pl., or inspired saints, as the author of the medical work called suśruta, &c.

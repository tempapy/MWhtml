There are six schools or systems of philosophy recognised by the Hindus, viz. 1. Pūrva Mī- mānsā, pūrvvamīmāṃsāpūrvvamīmāṃsāpūrvamīmāṃsā, founded by Jaimini, for the purpose of illustrating and explaining the karmakānda, or ritual part of the Vedas. It gives the rationale or meaning of the ritual enjoined in those sacred books, as distinct from the theological or spiritual part of them, and acknow- ledges the principle of ekatvaṃekatvaekatvam, or oneness, which it was the object of the Vedānta system more fully to investi- gate. 2. Sānkhya, sāṃkhyaḥsāṃkhya, ascribed to the Muni Kapila. This system is an attempt to account for the existence of the universe without the Deity, by asserting the principle of dvaitaṃdvaitadvaitam or duality, or, in other words, the co-ex- istence of Spirit and matter. 3. Vedānta, vedāntaḥvedānta, or Uttara Mīmānsā, uttaramīmāṃsāuttaramīmāṃsā, said to have been founded by Vyāsa, and subsequently fully developed by its most celebrated teacher śaṅkaraḥśaṅkara or śaṅkarācāryyaḥśaṅkarācāryyaśaṅkarācārya. This system is wholly concerned with the theological and philosophical part of the Vedas, or that part contained in the chapters called Upanishads, and its teaching is intended to oppose the duality of the Sānkhya system by shewing that the end or aim of the Vedas is ekatvaṃekatvaekatvam, or the oneness of Spirit. It denies the co-existence of matter with Spirit, and hence the conclusion arrived at is the non-existence of any thing but God. 4. Nyāya, nyāyaḥnyāya, founded by the inspired sage Gautama, and the most popular of all the systems. It is neither so mystical as the Vedānta, nor so fanciful as the Sānkhya. Its title signifies ‘the fitness of things,’ and it may be described as an attempt to treat of almost all subjects, according to a philosophical method and logical arrangement. 5. Vaiśeṣika, vaiśeṣikaṃvaiśeṣikavaiśeṣikam, a branch of the Nyāya, founded by the sage kaṇādaḥkaṇāda, to supply what was incomplete in the aphorisms of Gautama. 6. Yoga, yogaḥyoga or Pātanjala, pātañjalaṃpātañjalapātañjalam, founded by the sage patañjaliḥpatañjali m. Its object is to shew that, even in the body, there may be, to a great extent, union with the Supreme Being. These philosophical systems are contained in sacred writings called Ṣāstras. See the word.

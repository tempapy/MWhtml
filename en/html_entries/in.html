IN, prep. nini prefixed to roots may give the sense of ‘in;’ as, ‘to enter in,’ niviśniviś; ‘to dwell in,’ nivasnivas. When ‘in’ is used for ‘within,’ or ‘in the midst,’ it may be expressed by antarantar, antarāantarā, antareantare, madhyemadhye. But the most frequent use of ‘in’ is expressed in Sanskrit by the locative case, without any preposition; as, ‘in the city,’ nagarenagare; ‘in the night,’ rātraurātrau; ‘a tree planted in the earth,’ pṛthivyāṃpṛthivyām ropitoropito vṛkṣaḥvṛkṣa; ‘confidence in you,’ tvayitvayi viśvāsaḥviśvāsa; ‘engaging in the acquisition of wealth,’ arthārjanearthārjane pravṛttiḥpravṛtti f.; ‘the sun in the east,’ prāciprāci sūryyaḥsūryyasūrya; ‘he sinks in the mud,’ paṅkepaṅke majjatimajjati; ‘he delights in the good of others,’ parahiteparahite ramateramate; ‘in the front of the fight,’ raṇamūrddhiraṇamūrddhi. In cases where ‘in’ refers to any division of time, or the manner in which any thing is done, it is usually expressed in Sanskrit by the instr. case; as, ‘in three years,’ tribhirtribhir varṣaiḥvarṣai; ‘in twelve months,’ dvādaśabhirdvādaśabhir māsaiḥmāsai; ‘in hundreds of years,’ varṣaśataiḥvarṣaśatai; ‘in an instant,’ kṣaṇenakṣaṇena; ‘in process of time,’ kālaparyyāyeṇakālaparyyāyeṇakālaparyāyeṇa; ‘in how long time?’ kiyatākiyatā kālenakālena; ‘in a short time,’ acireṇaacireṇa; ‘in this manner,’ anenaanena prakāreṇaprakāreṇa; ‘in abundance,’ bāhulyenabāhulyena; ‘in the form of a man,’ nararūpeṇanararūpeṇa; ‘in joke,’ parihāsenaparihāsena; ‘written in Sanskrit,’ saṃskṛtenasaṃskṛtena racitaṃracitaracitam. Certain adverbial affixes and prefixes may often express ‘in;’ as, ‘in front,’ agratasagratas; ‘in the first place,’ prathamatasprathamatas; ‘in another place,’ anyatraanyatra; ‘in one place,’ ekatraekatra; ‘in many places,’ bahutrabahutra; ‘in the next world,’ amutraamutra; ‘in every way,’ sarvvathāsarvvathāsarvathā; ‘in ano- ther way,’ anyathāanyathā; ‘in one way,’ ekadhāekadhā; ‘in a hundred ways,’ śatadhāśatadhā; ‘in many ways,’ bahudhābahudhā; ‘in some way,’ kathañcitkathañcit, kathañcanakathañcana; ‘in small quantities,’ alpaśasalpaśas; ‘in large quantities,’ bahuśasbahuśas; ‘in proper order,’ kramaśaskramaśas, kramataskramatas, anukramaṃanukramaanukramam; ‘in every direction,’ pratideśaṃpratideśapratideśam; ‘in three direc- tions,’ tredhātredhā; ‘in like manner,’ tathaivatathaiva, tadvattadvat; ‘in vain,’ vṛthāvṛthā, mudhāmudhā. Sometimes ‘in’ may be expressed by a par- ticiple; as, ‘he has pleasure in calumniating others,’ anyānanyān parivadanparivadan sasa tupyatitupyati or parābhiśaṃsanaṃparābhiśaṃsanaparābhiśaṃsanam kṛtvākṛtvā tuṣṭotuṣṭo bhavatibhavati; ‘what bravery is there in killing a sleeping man?’ kiṃkim pauruṣaṃpauruṣapauruṣam hatvāhatvā suptaṃsuptasuptam; ‘he said that to me in joke,’ upahasyaupahasya māmmām tadtad avadatavadat. The sense of ‘in,’ may be inherent in a noun; as, ‘to go in and out,’ gamanāgamanegamanāgamane kṛkṛ. ‘In as much as,’ is ex- pressed by yasmātyasmāt, yenayena, or by the abl. or instr. case of the abstract noun; as, ‘in as much as it is founded on observation,’ pratyakṣamūlatvātpratyakṣamūlatvāt or pratyakṣamūlatayāpratyakṣamūlatayā.

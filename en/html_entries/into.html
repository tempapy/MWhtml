INTO, prep. madhye, antar, antare, abhyantare, antareṇa; ‘into the forest,’ araṇyamadhye. When ‘into’ follows verbs of motion it may be expressed by the accus. c.; as, ‘come into the house,’ gṛham āyāhi; ‘the river runs into the ocean,’ nadī samudraṃsamudrasamudram viśati. When it follows verbs denoting examination or inspection it may also be expressed by the acc. c.; as, ‘he looks into the letter,’ patraṃpatrapatram nirūpayati. When it follows verbs denoting insertion or inclusion it may be expressed by the loc. c.; as, ‘he inserts the wedge into the wood,’ kīlaṃkīlakīlam kāṣṭhe niveśayati. When it follows verbs denoting mixture it may be expressed by the instrum. c., or by saha with the instrum. c.; as, ‘he puts the wine into the water,’ madyaṃmadyamadyam jalena saha miśrayati. When ‘into’ denotes the passing of a thing from one state to another it may be expressed by the affixed sāt; as, ‘converted into water,’ jalasāskṛtaḥjalasāskṛta -tātā -taṃtatamtaṃ, or by changing the final of the noun to ī; as, ‘reduced into one mass,’ piṇḍībhūtaḥpiṇḍībhūta -tātā -taṃtatamtaṃ. When ‘into’ is used for ‘in’ it may usually be expressed by the loc. c.; as, ‘into the ear,’ karṇe. This last expres- sion is also rendered by upakarṇaṃupakarṇaupakarṇam.

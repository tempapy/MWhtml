SYLLOGISM, s. nyāyaḥnyāya, avayavīavayavī m. (n)n, avayavaghaṭitavākyaṃavayavaghaṭitavākyaavayavaghaṭitavākyam, avayavayukta-avayavayuktaavayavayukta vākyaṃvākyavākyam, avayavopetavākyaṃavayavopetavākyaavayavopetavākyam, ānupūrvvaṃānupūrvvaānupūrvvamānupūrvaānupūrvam. A syllogism has five mem- bers or avayavāḥavayavā, according to the Nyāya system, viz. 1. The proposition or assertion to be proved, pratijñāpratijñā; 2. the reason given, hetuḥhetu; 3. the example or illustration, udāharaṇaṃudāharaṇaudāharaṇam; 4. the application, upanayaḥupanaya; 5. the conclusion, nigamanaṃnigamananigamanam. Thus, 1. ‘The hill is fiery,’ parvvatoparvvatoparvato vahnimānvahnimān. 2. ‘Because it smokes,’ dhūmavattvātdhūmavattvāt. 3. ‘Whatever smokes is fiery, like a culinary hearth,’ yoyo yoyo dhūmavāndhūmavān sasa vahnimānvahnimān yathāyathā mahānasaḥmahānasa. 4. ‘This (hill) has smoke which is always accompanied with fire,’ vahni-vahnivahni vyāpyadhūmavānvyāpyadhūmavān ayaṃayaayam. 5. ‘Therefore it is fiery,’ tasmādtasmād vahnimānvahnimān. According to the Mīmānsā school, a syllogism has only the last three members.

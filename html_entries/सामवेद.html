• sāma—veda m. ‘Veda of chants’, N. of one of the three principal Vedas (See veda) [1205,2]
  • it contains a number of verses or stanzas nearly all of which [except about 78] occur in the Ṛg-veda and which, modified in various ways, are chanted, mostly, by the Udgātṛ priests at Soma sacrifices [1205,2]
  • the Saṃhitā of the Sāma-vedasāma-vedasāmaveda consists of two parts [1205,2]
  • the first, called Ārcika [or Pūrvârcika or Chando-granthachando-granthachandograntha], contains 585 verses disjoined from their proper sequence in the Ṛg-vedaṛg-vedaṛgveda and arranged in 59 Daśatis or decades, which again are subdivided into Prapāṭhakas and Ardha-prapāṭhakaardha-prapāṭhakaardhaprapāṭhakas [1205,2]
  • the second, called Uttarârcika or Uttarā-granthauttarā-granthauttarāgrantha, contains 1225 verses, also chiefly from the Ṛk-saṃhitāṛk-saṃhitāṛksaṃhitā, but less disjointed than in the first part, and arranged in nine Prapāṭhakas with Ardha-prapāṭhakaardha-prapāṭhakaardhaprapāṭhakas, mostly, however, grouped in triplets [1205,2]
  • the directions for the formation of Sāmans or chants out of these verses are carefully laid down in the Gānas or manuals for chanting, two of which, viz. the Geya-gānageya-gānageyagāna and Āraṇya-gānaāraṇya-gānaāraṇyagāna, are a directory for the Ārcika portion, and two, viz. Ūha-gānaūha-gānaūhagāna and Ūhya-gānaūhya-gānaūhyagāna, for the Uttarârcikā [1205,2]
  • in Mn. i, 23 the Sāma-vedasāma-vedasāmaveda is described as drawn forth from the sun [1205,2]
  • in iv, 124 it is described as having a special reference to the Pitṛs or deceased ancestors, and its sound is therefore said to possess a kind of impurity, whereas the Ṛg-veda has the gods for his objects and the Yajurveda men [1205,2]
  • the Sāma-vedasāma-vedasāmaveda is said to possess 8 Brāhmaṇas [see brāhmaṇa] Br.; ŚāṅkhŚr. &c. ([IW. 25]) [1205,2]

  • n. (accord. to some also m.) N. of one of the three great divisions of Hindū philosophy (ascribed to the sage Kapilakapila [q.v.], and so called either from ‘discriminating’, in general, or, more probably, from ‘reckoning up’ or ‘enumerating’ twenty-five Tattvatattvas [See tattvatattva] or true entities [twenty-three of which are evolved out of Prakṛtiprakṛti ‘the primordial Essence’ or ‘first-Producer’], viz. Buddhibuddhi, Ahaṃkāraahaṃkāra, the five Tan-mātratanmātratanmātras, the five Mahā-bhūtamahābhūtamahābhūtas and Manasmanas; the twenty-fifth being Puruṣapuruṣa or Spirit [sometimes called Soul] which is neither a Producer nor Production [see vikāravikāra], but wholly distinct from the twenty-four other Tattvatattvas, and is multitudinous, each separate Puruṣapuruṣa by its union with Prakṛtiprakṛti causing a separate creation out of Prakṛtiprakṛti, the object of the philosophy being to effect the final liberation of the Puruṣapuruṣa or Spirit from the fetters caused by that creation; the Yogayoga [q.v.] branch of the Sāṃkhyasāṃkhya recognizes a Supreme Spirit dominating each separate Puruṣapuruṣa; the Tantratantras identify Prakṛtiprakṛti with the wives of the gods, esp. with the wife of Śivaśiva; the oldest systematic exposition of the Sāṃkhyasāṃkhya seems to have been by an author called Pañca-śikhapañcaśikhapañcaśikha [the germ, however, being found in the Ṣaṣṭi-tantraṣaṣṭitantraṣaṣṭitantra, of which only scanty fragments are extant]; the original Sūtrasūtras were superseded by the Sāṃkhya-kārikāsāṃkhyakārikāsāṃkhyakārikā of Īśvara-kṛṣṇaīśvarakṛṣṇaīśvarakṛṣṇa, the oldest manual on the Sāṃkhyasāṃkhya system that has come down to us and probably written in the 5th century A.D., while the Sāṃkhyasāṃkhya-sūtrasūtras or Śiva-pravacanaśivapravacanaśivapravacana and Tattva-samāsatattvasamāsatattvasamāsa, ascribed to the sage Kapilakapila, are now thought to belong to as late a date as the 14th or 15th century or perhaps a little later), ŚvetUp.; MBh. &c., IW. 73 &c.; RTL.

• viṣṇuviṣṇu
  • m. (prob. fr. √viṣviṣ, ‘All-pervader’ or ‘Worker’) N. of one of the principal Hindū deities (in the later mythology regarded as ‘the preserver’, and with Brahmābrahmā ‘the creator’ and Śivaśiva ‘the destroyer’, constituting the well-known Tri-mūrtitrimūrtitrimūrti or triad; although Viṣṇuviṣṇu comes second in the triad he is identified with the supreme deity by his worshippers; in the Vedic period, however, he is not placed in the foremost rank, although he is frequently invoked with other gods [esp. with Indraindra whom he assists in killing Vṛtravṛtra and with whom he drinks the Somasoma juice; cf. his later names Indrânujaindrānuja and Upêndraupendra]; as distinguished from the other Vedic deities, he is a personification of the light and of the sun, esp. in his striding over the heavens, which he is said to do in three paces [See tri-vikramatrivikramatrivikrama and cf. balibali, vāmanavāmana], explained as denoting the threefold manifestations of light in the form of fire, lightning, and the sun, or as designating the three daily stations of the sun in his rising, culminating, and setting ; Viṣṇuviṣṇu does not appear to have been included at first among the Ādityaādityas [q.v.], although in later times he is accorded the foremost place among them; in the Brāhmaṇabrāhmaṇas he is identified with sacrifice, and in one described as a dwarf; in the Mahā-bhāratamahābhāratamahābhārata and Rāmâyaṇarāmāyaṇa he rises to the supremacy which in some places he now enjoys as the most popular deity of modern Hindū worship; the great rivalry between him and Śivaśiva [cf. vaiṣṇavavaiṣṇava and śaivaśaiva] is not fully developed till the period of the Purāṇapurāṇas: the distinguishing feature in the character of the Post-vedic Viṣṇuviṣṇu is his condescending to become incarnate in a portion of his essence on ten principal occasions, to deliver mankind from certain great dangers [cf. avatāraavatāra and, IW. 327]; some of the Purāṇapurāṇas make 22 incarnations, or even 24, instead of 10; the Vaiṣṇavavaiṣṇavas regard Viṣṇuviṣṇu as the supreme being, and often identify him with Nārāyaṇanārāyaṇa, the personified Puruṣapuruṣa or primeval living spirit [described as moving on the waters, reclining on Śeṣaśeṣa, the serpent of infinity, while the god Brahmābrahmā emerges from a lotus growing from his navel; cf. Manu i, 10]; the wives of Viṣṇuviṣṇu are Aditiaditi and Sinīvālīsinīvālī, later Lakṣmīlakṣmī or Śrīśrī and even Sarasvatīsarasvatī; his son is Kāma-devakāmadevakāmadeva, god of love, and his paradise is called Vaikuṇṭhavaikuṇṭha; he is usually represented with a peculiar mark on his breast called Śrī-vatsaśrīvatsaśrīvatsa, and as holding a śaṅkhaśaṅkha, or conch-shell called Pāñcajanyapāñcajanya, a cakracakra or quoit-like missile-weapon called Su-darśanasudarśanasudarśana, a gadāgadā or club called Kaumodakīkaumodakī and a padmapadma or lotus; he has also a bow called Śārṅgaśārṅga, and a sword called Nandakanandaka; his vāhanavāhana or vehicle is Garuḍagaruḍa q.v.; he has a jewel on his wrist called Syamantakasyamantaka, another on his breast called Kaustubhakaustubha, and the river Ganges is said to issue from his foot; the demons slain by him in his character of ‘preserver from evil’, or by Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa as identified with him, are Madhumadhu, Dhenukadhenuka, Cāṇūracāṇūra, Yamalayamala, and Arjunaarjuna [see yamalārjunayamalārjuna], Kāla-nemikālanemikālanemi, Haya-grīvahayagrīvahayagrīva, Śakaṭaśakaṭa, Ariṣṭaariṣṭa, Kaiṭabhakaiṭabha, Kaṉsakaṃsa, Keśinkeśin, Muramura, Śālvaśālva, Maindamainda, Dvi-vidadvividadvivida, Rāhurāhu, Hiraṇya-kaśipuhiraṇyakaśipuhiraṇyakaśipu, Bāṇabāṇa, Kāliyakāliya, Narakanaraka, Balibali; he is worshipped under a thousand names, which are all enumerated in MBh. xiii, 6950-7056; he is sometimes regarded as the divinity of the lunar mansion called Śravaṇaśravaṇa), RV. &c. &c. (cf. RTL. 44; IW. 324) [999,1]

• sītāsītā
  • f. (less correctly written śītāśītā; cf. sīmansīman, sīrasīra) a furrow, the track or line of a ploughshare (also personified, and apparently once worshipped as a kind of goddess resembling Pomona; in RV. iv, 57, 6, Sītāsītā is invoked as presiding over agriculture or the fruits of the earth; in VS. xii, 69-72, Sītāsītā ‘the Furrow’ is again personified and addressed, four furrows being required to be drawn at the ceremony when the above stanzas are recited; in TBr. she is called sāvitrīsāvitrī, and in PārGṛ. indra-patnīindrapatnīindrapatnī, ‘the wife of Indraindra’; in epic poetry Sītāsītā is the wife of Rāmacandrarāmacandra and daughter of Janakajanaka, king of Mithilāmithilā, capital of Videhavideha, who was otherwise called Sīradhvajasīradhvaja; she was named Sītāsītā because fabled to have sprung from a furrow made by Janakajanaka while ploughing the ground to prepare it for a sacrifice instituted by him to obtain progeny, whence her epithet Ayoni-jāayonijāayonijā, ‘not womb-born’; her other common names, Maithilīmaithilī and Vaidehīvaidehī, are from the place of her birth; according to one legend she was Vedavatīvedavatī q.v., in the Kṛtakṛta age; accord. to others she was an incarnation of Lakṣmilakṣmi and of Umāumā; the story of Rāmarāma's bending the bow, which was to be the condition of the gift of Sītāsītā, is told in R. i, 67; Sītāsītā's younger sister Urmilāurmilā was at the same time given to Lakṣmaṇalakṣmaṇa, and two nieces of Janakajanaka, daughters of his brother king Kuśa-dhvajakuśadhvajakuśadhvaja, to Bharatabharata and Śatrughnaśatrughna), RV. &c. &c., IW. 335 n. 1; 337 &c. [1218,2]

  • of an Upaniṣadupaniṣad, Cat. [1218,2]

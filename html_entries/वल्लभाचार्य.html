• vallabhā̑cāryavallabhācārya
  • m. N. of a celebrated Vaiṣṇavavaiṣṇava teacher (successor of a less celebrated teacher Viṣṇusvāminviṣṇusvāmin; he was born, it is said, in the forest of Campâraṇyacampāraṇya in 1479; at an early age he travelling to propagate his doctrines, and at the court of Kṛṣṇadevakṛṣṇadeva, king of Vijaya-nagaravijayanagaravijayanagara, succeeded so well in his controversies with the Śaivaśaivas that the Vaiṣṇavavaiṣṇavas chose him as their chief; he then went to other parts of India, and finally settled down at Benares, where he composed seventeen works, the most important of which were a commentary on the Vedântavedānta and Mīmāṉsāmīmāṃsā Sūtrasūtras and another on the Bhāgavata-Purāṇabhāgavatapurāṇabhāgavatapurāṇa, on which last the sect rest their doctrines; he left behind eighty-four disciples, of each of whom some story is told, and these stories are often repeated on festive occasions. He taught a non-ascetical view of religion and deprecated all self-mortification as dishonouring the body which contained a portion of the supreme Spirit. His followers in Bombay and Gujarat, and their leaders, are called Mahā-rājamahārājamahārājas; they are called the epicureans of India), RTL. 134-137 [928,3]

• varuṇavaruṇa
  • m. (once in the TĀr. varuṇavaruṇa) ‘All-enveloping Sky’, N. of an Ādityaāditya (in the Vedaveda commonly associated with Mitramitra [q.v.] and presiding over the night as Mitramitra over the day, but often celebrated separately, whereas Mitramitra is rarely invoked alone; Varuṇavaruṇa is one of the oldest of the Vedic gods, and is commonly thought to correspond to the Οὐρανός of the Greeks, although of a more spiritual conception; he is often regarded as the supreme deity, being then styled ‘king of the gods’ or ‘king of both gods and men’ or ‘king of the universe’; no other deity has such grand attributes and functions assigned to him; he is described as fashioning and upholding heaven and earth, as possessing extraordinary power and wisdom called māyāmāyā, as sending his spies or messengers throughout both worlds, as numbering the very winkings of men's eyes, as hating falsehood, as seizing transgressors with his pāśapāśa or noose, as inflicting diseases, especially dropsy, as pardoning sin, as the guardian of immortality; he is also invoked in the Vedaveda together with Indraindra, and in later Vedic literature together with Agniagni, with Yamayama, and with Viṣṇuviṣṇu; in RV. iv, 1, 2, he is even called the brother of Agniagni; though not generally regarded in the Vedaveda as a god of the ocean, yet he is often connected with the waters, especially the waters of the atmosphere or firmament, and in one place [RV. vii, 64, 2] is called with Mitramitra, sindhu-patisindhupatisindhupati, ‘lord of the sea or of rivers’; hence in the later mythology he became a kind of Neptune, and is there best known in his character of god of the ocean; in the MBh. Varuṇavaruṇa is said to be a son of Kardamakardama and father of Puṣkarapuṣkara, and is also variously represented as one of the Deva-gandharvadevagandharvadevagandharvas, as a Nāganāga, as a king of the Nāganāgas, and as an Asuraasura; he is the regent of the western quarter [cf. loka-pālalokapālalokapāla] and of the Nakṣatranakṣatra Śatabhiṣajśatabhiṣaj [VarBṛS.]; the Jainajainas consider Varuṇavaruṇa as a servant of the twentieth Arhatarhat of the present Avasarpiṇīavasarpiṇī), RV. &c. &c. (cf. IW. 10; 12 &c.) [921,2]

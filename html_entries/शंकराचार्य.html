• śaṃkarā̑cārya a See below. [1055,1]
  • śaṃkarā̑cārya b m. N. of various teachers and authors, (esp.) of a celebrated teacher of the Vedânta philosophy and reviver of Brāhmanism (he is thought to have lived between A.D. 788 and 820, but according to tradition he flourished 200 B.C., and was a native of Kerala or Malabar; all accounts describe him as having led an erratic controversial life; his learning and sanctity were in such repute that he was held to have been an incarnation of Śiva, and to have worked various miracles; he is said to have died at the age of thirty-two, and to have had four principal disciples, called Padma-pādapadma-pādapadmapāda, Hastâmalaka, Sureśvara or Mandana, and Troṭaka; another of his disciples, Ānanda-giriānanda-giriānandagiri, wrote a history of his controversial exploits, called Śaṃkara-vijayaśaṃkara-vijayaśaṃkaravijaya q.v.; tradition makes him the founder of one of the principal Śaiva sects, the Daśa-nāmi-Daṇḍindaśa-nāmi-daṇḍindaśanāmidaṇḍins or ‘Ten-named Mendicants’ RTL. 87; he is the reputed author of a large number of original works, such as the Ātma-bodhaātma-bodhaātmabodha, Ānanda-laharīānanda-laharīānandalaharī, Jñāna-bodhinījñāna-bodhinījñānabodhinī, Maṇi-ratna-mālāmaṇi-ratna-mālāmaṇiratnamālā, &c.; and commentaries on the Upaniṣads, and on the Brahma-mīmāṉsābrahma-mīmāṃsābrahmamīmāṃsā or Vedānta-sūtravedānta-sūtravedāntasūtra, Bhagavadgītā, and Mahā-bhāratamahā-bhāratamahābhārata, &c.), IW. 46; RTL. 53 [1055,1]

  • śaṃkarācāryaśaṃkarācārya
  • m. N. of various teachers and authors, (esp.) of a celebrated teacher of the Vedântavedānta philosophy and reviver of Brāhmanism (he is thought to have lived between A.D. 788 and 820, but according to tradition he flourished 200 B.C., and was a native of Keralakerala or Malabar; all accounts describe him as having led an erratic controversial life; his learning and sanctity were in such repute that he was held to have been an incarnation of Śivaśiva, and to have worked various miracles; he is said to have died at the age of thirty-two, and to have had four principal disciples, called Padma-pādapadmapādapadmapāda, Hastâmalakahastāmalaka, Sureśvarasureśvara or Mandanamandana, and Troṭakatroṭaka; another of his disciples, Ānanda-giriānandagiriānandagiri, wrote a history of his controversial exploits, called Śaṃkara-vijayaśaṃkaravijayaśaṃkaravijaya q.v.; tradition makes him the founder of one of the principal Śaivaśaiva sects, the Daśa-nāmi-Daṇḍindaśanāmidaṇḍindaśanāmidaṇḍins or ‘Ten-named Mendicants’ RTL. 87; he is the reputed author of a large number of original works, such as the Ātma-bodhaātmabodhaātmabodha, Ānanda-laharīānandalaharīānandalaharī, Jñāna-bodhinījñānabodhinījñānabodhinī, Maṇi-ratna-mālāmaṇiratnamālāmaṇiratnamālā, &c.; and commentaries on the Upaniṣadupaniṣads, and on the Brahma-mīmāṉsābrahmamīmāṃsābrahmamīmāṃsā or Vedānta-sūtravedāntasūtravedāntasūtra, Bhagavadgītābhagavadgītā, and Mahā-bhāratamahābhāratamahābhārata, &c.), IW. 46; RTL. 53 [1055,1]

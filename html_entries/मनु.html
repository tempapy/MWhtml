  • m. ‘the thinking creature (?)’, man, mankind, RV.; VS.; AitBr.; TĀr. (also as opp. to evil spirits, RV. i, 130, 8; viii, 98, 6 &c.; the Ṛbhuṛbhus are called manormanor napātaḥnapātaḥ, the sons of man, iii, 60, 3) [784,2]
  • the Man par excellence or the representative man and father of the human race (regarded in the RV. as the first to have instituted sacrifices and religious ceremonies, and associated with the Ṛṣiṛṣis Kaṇvakaṇva and Atriatri; in the AitBr. described as dividing his possessions among some of his sons to the exclusion of one called Nābhā-nediṣṭhanābhānediṣṭhanābhānediṣṭha q.v.; called Sāṃvaraṇasāṃvaraṇa as author of RV. ix, 101, 10-12; Āpsavaāpsava as author of ib. 106, 7-9; in Naigh. v, 6 he is numbered among the 31 divine beings of the upper sphere, and, VS. xi, 66 as father of men even identified with Prajā-patiprajāpatiprajāpati; but the name Manumanu is esp. applied to 14 successive mythical progenitors and sovereigns of the earth, described, Mn. i, 63 and in later works as creating and supporting this world through successive Antaraantaras or long periods of time See manv-antaramanvantaramanvantara below; the first is called Svāyambhuvasvāyambhuva as sprung from svayam-bhūsvayambhūsvayambhū, the Self-existent, and described in Mn. 12, 34 as a sort of secondary creator, who commenced his work by producing 10 Prajāpatiprajāpatis or Maharṣimaharṣis, of whom the first was marīcimarīci, Light; to this Manumanu is ascribed the celebrated ‘code of Manumanu’ See manu-saṃhitāmanusaṃhitāmanusaṃhitā, and two ancient Sūtrasūtra works on Kalpakalpa and Gṛhyagṛhya i.e. sacrificial and domestic rites; he is also called Hairaṇyagarbhahairaṇyagarbha as son of Hiraṇya-garbhahiraṇyagarbhahiraṇyagarbha, and Prācetasaprācetasa, as son of Pra-cetaspracetaspracetas; the next 5 Manumanus are called Svārociṣasvārociṣa, Auttamiauttami, Tāmasatāmasa, Raivataraivata, Cākṣuṣacākṣuṣa cf. IW. 208 n.1; the 7th Manumanu, called vaivasvatavaivasvata, Sun-born, or from his piety, satya-vratasatyavratasatyavrata, is regarded as the progenitor of the present race of living beings, and said, like the Noah of the Old Testament, to have been preserved from a great flood by Viṣṇuviṣṇu or Brahmābrahmā in the form of a fish: he is also variously described as one of the 12 Ādityaādityas, as the author of RV. viii, 27-31, as the brother of Yamayama, who as a son of the Sun is also called Vaivasvatavaivasvata, as the founder and first king of Ayodhyāayodhyā, and as father of Ilāilā who married Budhabudha, son of the Moon, the two great solar and lunar races being thus nearly related to each other See IW. 344; 373; the 8th Manumanu or first of the future Manumanus accord. to VP. iii, 2, will be Sāvarṇisāvarṇi; the 9th Dakṣa-sāvarṇidakṣasāvarṇidakṣasāvarṇi; the 10th Brahma-sāvarṇibrahmasāvarṇibrahmasāvarṇi; the 11th Dharma-sāvarṇidharmasāvarṇidharmasāvarṇi; the 12th Rudra-sāvarṇirudrasāvarṇirudrasāvarṇi; the 13th Raucyaraucya or Deva-sāvarṇidevasāvarṇidevasāvarṇi; the 14th Bhautyabhautya or Indraindra-sāvarṇisāvarṇi) [784,2]

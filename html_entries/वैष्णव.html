  • vaiṣṇavavaiṣṇava
  • m. patr. fr. viṣṇuviṣṇu g. bidādibidādi [1027,2]
  • ‘a worshipper of Viṣṇuviṣṇu’ N. of one of the three great divisions of modern Hindūism (the other two being the Śaivaśaivas and Śāktaśāktas; the Vaiṣṇavavaiṣṇavas identify Viṣṇuviṣṇu - rather than Brahmābrahmā and Śivaśiva - with the supreme Being, and are exclusively devoted to his worship; they have become separated into four principal and some minor sects, as follow: 1. the Rāmânujarāmānujas, founded by Rāmânujarāmānuja, who is said to have lived for 120 years [from 1017 till 1137 A.D.] ; his chief doctrines are described at p. 878, col. 1, and in RTL. p.119 &c.; one peculiarity of his sect is the scrupulous preparation and privacy of three meals; 2. the Mādhvamādhvas, founded by a Kanarese Brāhman named Madhvamadhva, whose chief doctrines are described at p. 782, col. 3, and in RTL. p.130 &c.; 3. the Vallabhavallabhas, founded by Vallabhâcāryavallabhācārya, whose chief doctrines are described at p. 928, col. 3, and in RTL. p.134 &c.; 4. a sect in Bengal founded by Caitanyacaitanya [q.v.] who was regarded by his followers as an incarnation of Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa; his chief doctrine was the duty of bhaktibhakti, or love for that god which was to be so strong that no caste-feelings could exist with it [see, RTL. p.140 &c.] Of the minor Vaiṣṇavavaiṣṇava sects those founded by Nimbârkanimbārka or Nimbâdityanimbāditya [RTL. 146] and by Rāmânandarāmānanda [RTL. 147] and by Svāmi-Nārāyaṇasvāminārāyaṇasvāminārāyaṇa [RTL. 148] are perhaps the most important, to which also may be added the reformed theistic sect founded by Kabīr [RTL. 158] and the Sikh theistic sect founded by Nānak [RTL. 161]) [1027,2]

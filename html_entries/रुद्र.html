  • rudrarudra
  • m. ‘Roarer or Howler’, N. of the god of tempests and father and ruler of the Rudrarudras and Marutmaruts (in the Vedaveda he is closely connected with Indraindra and still more with Agniagni, the god of fire, which, as a destroying agent, rages and crackles like the roaring storm, and also with Kālakāla or Time the all-consumer, with whom he is afterwards identified; though generally represented as a destroying deity, whose terrible shafts bring death or disease on men and cattle, he has also the epithet śivaśiva, ‘benevolent’ or ‘auspicious’, and is even supposed to possess healing powers from his chasing away vapours and purifying the atmosphere; in the later mythology the word śivaśiva, which does not occur as a name in the Vedaveda, was employed, first as an euphemistic epithet and then as a real name for Rudrarudra, who lost his special connection with storms and developed into a form of the disintegrating and reintegrating principle; while a new class of beings, described as eleven [or thirty-three] in number, though still called Rudrarudras, took the place of the original Rudrarudras or Marutmaruts: in VP. i, 7, Rudrarudra is said to have sprung from Brahmābrahmā's forehead, and to have afterwards separated himself into a figure half male and half female, the former portion separating again into the 11 Rudrarudras, hence these later Rudrarudras are sometimes regarded as inferior manifestations of Śivaśiva, and most of their names, which are variously given in the different Purāṇapurāṇas, are also names of Śivaśiva ; those of the VāyuP. are Ajaikapādajaikapād, Ahir-budhnyaahirbudhnyaahirbudhnya, Harahara, Nirṛtanirṛta, Īśvaraīśvara, Bhuvanabhuvana, Aṅgārakaaṅgāraka, Ardha-ketuardhaketuardhaketu, Mṛtyumṛtyu, Sarpasarpa, Kapālinkapālin;

  • vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha
  • m. (wrongly written vaśiṣṭhavaśiṣṭha), ‘the most wealthy’, N. of a celebrated Vedic Ṛṣiṛṣi or sage (owner of the ‘cow of plenty’, called Nandinīnandinī, offspring of Surabhisurabhi, which by granting all desires made him, as his name implies, master of every vasuvasu or desirable object; he was the typical representative of Brāhmanical rank, and the legends of his conflict with Viśvā-mitraviśvāmitraviśvāmitra, who raised himself from the kingly or Kṣatriyakṣatriya to the Brāhmanical class, were probably founded on the actual struggles which took place between the Brāhmans and Kṣatriyakṣatriyas; a great many hymns of the RV. are ascribed to these two great rivals; those of the seventh Maṇḍalamaṇḍala, besides some others, being attributed to Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha, while those of the third Maṇḍalamaṇḍala are assigned to Viśvā-mitraviśvāmitraviśvāmitra; in one of Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha's hymns he is represented as king Su-dāssudāssudās's family priest, an office to which Viśvā-mitraviśvāmitraviśvāmitra also aspired; in another hymn Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha claims to have been inspired by Varuṇavaruṇa, and in another [RV. vii, 33, 11] he is called the son of the Apsarasapsaras Urvaśīurvaśī by Mitramitra and Varuṇavaruṇa, whence his patronymic Maitrāvaruṇimaitrāvaruṇi ; in Manumanu, i, 35, he is enumerated among the ten Prajā-patiprajāpatiprajāpatis or Patriarchs produced by Manumanu Svāyambhuvasvāyambhuva for the peopling of the universe; in the MBh. he is mentioned as the family priest of the solar race or family of Ikṣvākuikṣvāku and Rāma-candrarāmacandrarāmacandra, and in the Purāṇapurāṇas as one of the arrangers of the Vedavedas in the Dvāparadvāpara age; he is, moreover, called the father of Aurvaaurva [Hariv.], of the Sukālinsukālins [Mn.], of seven sons [Hariv.; Pur.], and the husband of Akṣa-mālāakṣamālāakṣamālā or Arundhatīarundhatī [MBh.] and of Ūrjāūrjā [Pur.]; other legends make him one of the 7 patriarchal sages regarded as forming the Great Bear in which he represents the star ζ See ṛṣiṛṣi), RV. &c. &c. (cf. IW. 361; 402 n.1 &c.) [930,2]

  • the number five as symbolical of the five senses. (In addition to the five organs of perception, buddhī̑ndriyāṇibuddhīndriyāṇi or jñānȇndriyāṇijñānendriyāṇi, i.e. eye, ear, nose, tongue, and skin, the Hindūs enumerate five organs of action, karmȇndriyāṇikarmendriyāṇi i.e. larynx, hand, foot, anus, and parts of generation; between these ten organs and the soul or ātmanātman stands manasmanas or mind, considered as an eleventh organ; in the Vedāntavedānta, manasmanas, buddhibuddhi, ahaṃkāraahaṃkāra, and cittacitta form the four inner or internal organs, antar-indriyāṇiantarindriyāṇiantarindriyāṇi, so that according to this reckoning the organs are fourteen in number, each being presided over by its own ruler or niyantṛniyantṛ; thus, the eye by the Sun, the ear by the Quarters of the world, the nose by the two Aśvinaśvins, the tongue by Pracetaspracetas, the skin by the Wind, the voice by Fire, the hand by Indraindra, the foot by Viṣṇuviṣṇu, the anus by Mitramitra, the parts of generation by Prajāpatiprajāpati, manas by the Moon, buddhibuddhi by Brahmanbrahman, ahaṃkāraahaṃkāra by Śivaśiva, citta by Viṣṇuviṣṇu as Acyutaacyuta; in the Nyāyanyāya philosophy each organ is connected with its own peculiar element, the nose with the Earth, the tongue with Water, the eye with Light or Fire, the skin with Air, the ear with Ether; the Jainajainas divide the whole creation into five sections, according to the number of organs attributed to each being.)

• 1. kaka
    • the first consonant of the alphabet, and the first guttural letter (corresponding in sound to k in keep or king). [240,2]
  • kaka 2
    • kaskaska, kākāka, kimkim, interrog. pron. (See kimkim and 2. kadkad, and cf. the following words in which the interrogative base kaka appears, katamakatama, katarakatara, katikati, kathamkatham, kadākadākadakad, karhikarhi, kākāka, &c.), who? which? what? In its declension kaka follows the pronoun tadtad except in nom. acc. sing. neut., where kimkim has taken the place of kadkad or katkatkathkad in classical Sanskṛt; but the old form kadkad is found in the Veda (See Gram. 227); [240,2]
  • kaka
    • [cf. Zd. ka, kô, kâ, kat; Gk. πόθεν, πῶς, (Ion. κόθεν, κῶς,) τίς, τί; Lat. quis, quid; Lith. kas kaka; Goth. hvas, hvô, hva, Angl.Sax. hwā, hwaet; Eng. who, what.] [240,2]
    • The interrogative sentence introduced by kaka is often terminated by itiiti (e.g. kasyaka sasa putraputra itiiti kathyatām, let it be said, ‘whose son is he?’), but itiiti may be omitted and the sentence lose its direct interrogative character (e.g. kasyaka sasa putroputra nana jñāyate, it is not known whose son he is). kaka with or without √1. asasa may express ‘how is it possible that?’ ‘what power have I, you, they, &c.?’ (e.g. keka mamamama dhanvino'nye, what can the other archers do against me? keka āvām paritrātum, what power have we to rescue you?) kaka is often connected with a demonstrative pron. (e.g. kokokukaska 'yamayamaya āyātiāyāti, who comes here?) or with the potential (e.g. kokokukaska hariṃhari nindet, who will blame Hari?) kaka is sometimes repeated (e.g. kaḥkaska kokokukaska 'tra, who is there? kānka kānka, whom? whom? i.e. which of them? cf. Gram. 54), and the repetition is often due to a kind of attraction (e.g. keṣāṃka kiṃkiṃkim śāstramśāstra adhyayanīyamadhyayanīya, which book is to be read by whom? Gram. 836. aa). When kimkim is connected with the inst. c. of a noun or with the indeclinable participle it may express ‘what is gained by doing so, &c.?’ (= ko'rthas); (e.g. kiṃkiṃkim vilambenavilamba, what is gained by delay? kimkim bahunābahu, what is the use of more words? dhanenadhana kiṃkiṃkim yoyuyasyaī nana dadātidadāti, what is the use of wealth to him who does not give? with inst. and gen., nīrujaḥnīrujanīruj kimkim auṣadhaiḥauṣadha, what is the use of medicine to the healthy?) [240,3]
    • kaka is often followed by the particles ivaiva, uuū, nāmanāma, nununū, vāvāvauū, svidsvid, some of which serve merely to generalize the interrogation (e.g. kimkim ivaiva etadetad, what can this be? kakakokukas uuū śravat, who can possibly hear? kokokukaska nāmanāma jānāti, who indeed knows? kokokukaska nvayam, who, pray, is this? kiṃkiṃkim nununū kāryamkārya, what is to be done? kokokukaska vāvāvauūvās devāddeva anyaḥanyaanī, who possibly other than a god? kasyaka svidsvid hṛdayaṃhṛdaya nā̑stināstinanāasti, of what person is there no heart?) [240,3]
    • kaka is occasionally used alone as an indefinite pronoun, especially in negative sentences (e.g. nana kasyaka kokokukaska vallabhaḥvallabha, no one is a favourite of any one; nā̑nyonanāanyaanī jānāti kaḥkaska, no one else knows; kathaṃkathaṃkatham sasa ghātayati kamkamka, how does he kill any one?) Generally, however, kaka is only made indefinite when connected with the particles caca, canacana, cidcid, vāvāvauū, and apiapiap, in which case kaka may sometimes be preceded by the relative yaya (e.g. yeyayā keka caca, any persons whatsoever; yasyaiya kasyaika caca devatāyaidevatā, to any deity whatsoever; yāniyayānī kānika caca mitrāṇimitra, any friends whatsoever; yatyat kiṃcakiṃca, whatever). The particle canacana, being composed of caca and nana, properly gives a negative force to the pronoun (e.g. yasmādyasmātya indrādindra ṛteṛteṛtaṛti kiṃcanakiṃcana, without which Indra there is nothing), but the negative sense is generally dropped (e.g. kaścanakaścana, any one; nana kaścanakaścana, no one), and a relative is sometimes connected with it (e.g. yatyat kiṃcanakiṃcana, anything whatsoever). Examples of cidcid with the interrogative are common; vāvāvauū and apiapiap are not so common, but the latter is often found in classical Sanskṛt (e.g. kaścid, any one; kecid, some; nana kaścid, no one; nana kiṃcidkiṃcid apiapiap, nothing whatsoever; yaḥyasyaī kaścid, any one whatsoever; kecit - kecit, some - others; yasminya kasminka vāvāvauūvās deśedeśa, in any country whatsoever; nana kokokukaska 'pi, no one; nana kimapi, nothing whatever). kaka may sometimes be used, like 2. kadkad, at the beginning of a compound. See ka-pūyakapūyakapūya, &c. [240,3]

  • 4. kaka
    • a Taddhita affix (much used in forming adjectives; it may also be added to nouns to express diminution, deterioration, or similarity e.g. putrakaputraka, a little son; aśvakaaśvaka, a bad horse or like a horse). [240,3]

• kaka
  • the first consonant of the alphabet, and the first guttural letter (corresponding in sound to k in keep or king). [240,2]
  • kaka
  • kaskas, kākā, kimkim, interrog. pron. (See kimkim and 2. kadkad, and cf. the following words in which the interrogative base kaka appears, katamakatama, katarakatara, katikati, kathamkatham, kadākadā, karhikarhi, kākā, &c.), who? which? what? In its declension kaka follows the pronoun tadtad except in nom. acc. sing. neut., where kimkim has taken the place of kadkad or katkat in classical Sanskṛt; but the old form kadkad is found in the Vedaveda (See Gram. 227); [240,2]
  • kaka
  • [cf. Zd. ka, kô, kâ, kat; Gk. πόθεν, πῶς, (Ion. κόθεν, κῶς,) τίς, τί; Lat. quis, quid; Lith. kas kaka; Goth. hvas, hvô, hva, Angl.Sax. hwā, hwaet; Eng. who, what.] [240,2]
  • The interrogative sentence introduced by kaka is often terminated by itiiti (e.g. kasyakasya sasa putraputra itiiti kathyatāmkathyatām, let it be said, ‘whose son is he?’), but itiiti may be omitted and the sentence lose its direct interrogative character (e.g. kasyakasya sasa putroputro nana jñāyatejñāyate, it is not known whose son he is). kaka with or without √1. asas may express ‘how is it possible that?’ ‘what power have I, you, they, &c.?’ (e.g. keke mamamama dhanvino'nyedhanvinonye, what can the other archers do against me? keke āvāmāvām paritrātumparitrātum, what power have we to rescue you?) kaka is often connected with a demonstrative pron. (e.g. koko 'yamyam āyātiāyāti, who comes here?) or with the potential (e.g. koko hariṃhariṃ nindetnindet, who will blame Harihari?) kaka is sometimes repeated (e.g. kaḥkaḥ koko 'tratra, who is there? kānkān kānkān, whom? whom? i.e. which of them? cf. Gram. 54), and the repetition is often due to a kind of attraction (e.g. keṣāṃkeṣāṃ kiṃkiṃ śāstramśāstram adhyayanīyamadhyayanīyam, which book is to be read by whom? Gram. 836. aa). When kimkim is connected with the inst. c. of a noun or with the indeclinable participle it may express ‘what is gained by doing so, &c.?’ (= ko'rthaskorthas); (e.g. kiṃkiṃ vilambenavilambena, what is gained by delay? kimkim bahunābahunā, what is the use of more words? dhanenadhanena kiṃkiṃ yoyo nana dadātidadāti, what is the use of wealth to him who does not give? with inst. and gen., nīrujaḥnīrujaḥ kimkim auṣadhaiḥauṣadhaiḥ, what is the use of medicine to the healthy?) [240,3]
  • kaka is often followed by the particles ivaiva, uu, nāmanāma, nunu, vāvā, svidsvid, some of which serve merely to generalize the interrogation (e.g. kimkim ivaiva etadetad, what can this be? kaka uu śravatśravat, who can possibly hear? koko nāmanāma jānātijānāti, who indeed knows? koko nvayamnvayam, who, pray, is this? kiṃkiṃ nunu kāryamkāryam, what is to be done? koko vāvā devāddevād anyaḥanyaḥ, who possibly other than a god? kasyakasya svidsvid hṛdayaṃhṛdayaṃ nā̑stināsti, of what person is there no heart?) [240,3]
  • kaka is occasionally used alone as an indefinite pronoun, especially in negative sentences (e.g. nana kasyakasya koko vallabhaḥvallabhaḥ, no one is a favourite of any one; nā̑nyonānyo jānātijānāti kaḥkaḥ, no one else knows; kathaṃkathaṃ sasa ghātayatighātayati kamkam, how does he kill any one?) Generally, however, kaka is only made indefinite when connected with the particles caca, canacana, cidcid, vāvā, and apiapi, in which case kaka may sometimes be preceded by the relative yaya (e.g. yeye keke caca, any persons whatsoever; yasyaiyasyai kasyaikasyai caca devatāyaidevatāyai, to any deity whatsoever; yāniyāni kānikāni caca mitrāṇimitrāṇi, any friends whatsoever; yatyat kiṃcakiṃca, whatever). The particle canacana, being composed of caca and nana, properly gives a negative force to the pronoun (e.g. yasmādyasmād indrādindrād ṛteṛte kiṃcanakiṃcana, without which Indraindra there is nothing), but the negative sense is generally dropped (e.g. kaścanakaścana, any one; nana kaścanakaścana, no one), and a relative is sometimes connected with it (e.g. yatyat kiṃcanakiṃcana, anything whatsoever). Examples of cidcid with the interrogative are common vāvā and apiapi are not so common, but the latter is often found in classical Sanskṛt (e.g. kaścidkaścid, any one; kecidkecid, some; nana kaścidkaścid, no one; nana kiṃcidkiṃcid apiapi, nothing whatsoever; yaḥyaḥ kaścidkaścid, any one whatsoever; kecitkecit - kecitkecit, some - others; yasminyasmin kasminkasmin vāvā deśedeśe, in any country whatsoever; nana koko 'pipi, no one; nana kimapikimapi, nothing whatever). kaka may sometimes be used, like 2. kadkad, at the beginning of a compound. See ka-pūyakapūyakapūya, &c. [240,3]

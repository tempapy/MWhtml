• omom ind.
    • (√avav, Uṇ. i, 141; originally oṃ = āṃ, which may be derived from āāa, BRD.), a word of solemn affirmation and respectful assent, sometimes translated by ‘yes, verily, so be it’ (and in this sense compared with Amen; it is placed at the commencement of most Hindū works, and as a sacred exclamation may be uttered [but not so as to be heard by ears profane] at the beginning and end of a reading of the Vedas or previously to any prayer; it is also regarded as a particle of auspicious salutation [Hail!]; omom appears first in the Upaniṣads as a mystic monosyllable, and is there set forth as the object of profound religious meditation, the highest spiritual efficacy being attributed not only to the whole word but also to the three sounds aa, uuū, m, of which it consists; in later times omom is the mystic name for the Hindū triad, and represents the union of the three gods, viz. a (Viṣṇu), uuū (Śiva), m (Brahmā); it may also be typical of the three Vedas; omom is usually called praṇavapraṇava, more rarely akṣaraakṣara, or ekākṣaraekākṣara, and only in later times oṃkāraoṃkāra), VS.; ŚBr.; ChUp. &c. [235,3]
    • (Buddhists place omom at the beginning of their vidyāvidyāvidya ṣaḍakṣarī or mystical formulary in six syllables [viz. omom maṇimaṇi padmepadmapadmā hūṃ]; according to, T. omom may be used in the following senses: praṇavepraṇavapraṇu, ārambheārambha, svīkāresvīkāra, anumatauanumataanumati, apā̑kṛtauapākṛtaapākṛtiapaakṛtaākṛtaākṛti, asvīkāreasvīkāra, maṅgalemaṅgalamaṅgalā, śubheśubhaśubhāśubh, jñeyejñeya, brahmaṇi; with preceding aa or āāa, the oou of omom does not form Vṛddhi (auauaiu), but Guṇa (oou), Pāṇ. vi, 1, 95.)

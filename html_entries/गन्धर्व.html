  • m. a Gandharvagandharva [though in later times the Gandharvagandharvas are regarded as a class, yet in RV. rarely more than one is mentioned; he is designated as the heavenly Gandharvagandharva (divyadivya g°gandharva, RV. ix, 86, 36 and x, 139, 5), and is also called Viśvā-vasuviśvāvasuviśvāvasu (RV. x, 85, 21 and 22; 139, 4 and 5) and Vāyu-keśavāyukeśavāyukeśa (in pl. RV. iii, 38, 6); his habitation is the sky, or the region of the air and the heavenly waters (RV. i, 22, 14; viii, 77, 5; ix, 85, 12; 86, 36; x, 10, 4; AV. ii, 2, 3); his especial duty is to guard the heavenly Somasoma (RV. ix, 83, 4 and 85, 12), which the gods obtain through his intervention (RV.; AV. vii, 73, 3; cf. RV. i, 22, 14); it is obtained for the human race by Indraindra, who conquers the Gandharvagandharva and takes it by force (RV. viii, 1, 11 and 77, 5); the heavenly Gandharvagandharva is supposed to be a good physician, because the Somasoma is considered as the best medicine; possibly, however, the word Somasoma originally denoted not the beverage so called, but the moon, and the heavenly Gandharvagandharva may have been the genius or tutelary deity of the moon; in one passage (RV. ix, 86, 36) the heavenly Gandharvagandharva and the Somasoma are identified; he is also regarded as one of the genii who regulate the course of the Sun's horses (i, 163, 2; x, 177, 2; cf. 135, 5); he knows and makes known the secrets of heaven and divine truths generally (x, 139, 5 and 6; AV. ii, 1, 2; xx, 128, 3; VS. xi, 1; xxxii, 9); he is the parent of the first pair of human beings, Yamayama and Yamīyamī (RV. x, 10, 4), and has a peculiar mystical power over women and a right to possess them (RV. x, 85, 21 and 22; 40 and 41); for this reason he is invoked in marriage ceremonies (AV. xiv, 2, 35 and 36); ecstatic states of mind and possession by evil spirits are supposed to be derived from the heavenly Gandharvagandharva (cf. -gṛhītagṛhītagṛhīta, -grahagrahagraha); the Gandharvagandharvas as a class have the same characteristic features as the one Gandharvagandharva; they live in the sky (RV.; AV.; ŚBr. xiv), guard the Somasoma (RV. ix, 113, 3; ŚBr. iii; AitBr. i, 27), are governed by Varuṇavaruṇa (just as the Apsarasaapsarasas are governed by Somasoma), ŚBr. xiii; ĀśvŚr. x, 7, 3, know the best medicines (AV. viii, 7, 23; VS. xii, 98), regulate the course of the asterisms (AV. xiii, 1, 23; BhP. iv, 29, 21; hence twenty-seven are mentioned, VS. ix, 7), follow after women and are desirous of intercourse with them (AV.; ŚBr. iii); as soon as a girl becomes marriageable, she belongs to Somasoma, the Gandharvagandharvas, and Agniagni (Gṛhyās. ii, 19 f.; Pañcat.; Suśr.); the wives of the Gandharvagandharvas are the Apsarasaapsarasas (cf. gandharvā̑psarasgandharvāpsaras), and like them the Gandharvagandharvas are invoked in gambling with dice (AV. vii, 109, 5); they are also feared as evil beings together with the Rākṣasarākṣasas, Kimīdinkimīdins, Piśācapiśācas, &c., amulets being worn as a protection against them (AV.; Suśr.); they are said to have revealed the Vedavedas to Vācvāc (ŚBr. iii; cf. PārGṛ. ii, 12, 2), and are called the preceptors of the Ṛṣiṛṣis (ŚBr. xi); Purūravaspurūravas is called among them (ib.); in epic poetry the Gandharvagandharvas are the celestial musicians or heavenly singers (cf. RV. x, 177, 2) who form the orchestra at the banquets of the gods, and they belong together with the Apsarasaapsarasas to Indraindra's heaven, sharing also in his battles (Yājñ. i, 71; MBh.; Hariv. &c.; cf. RTL. p.238); in the more systematic mythology the Gandharvagandharvas constitute one of the classes into which the higher creation is divided (i.e. gods, manes, Gandharvagandharvas, AV. xi, 5, 2; or gods, Asuraasuras, Gandharvagandharvas, men, TS. vii, 8, 25, 2; cf. ŚBr. x; or gods, men, Gandharvagandharvas, Apsarasaapsarasas, Sarpasarpas, and manes, AitBr. iii, 31, 5; for other enumerations cf. Nir. iii, 8; Mn. i, 37 [RTL. p. 237] & iii, 196; vii, 23; xii, 47; Nal. &c.); divine and human Gandharvagandharvas are distinguished (TUp. ii, 8; the divine or Deva-Gandharvadevagandharvadevagandharvas are enumerated, MBh. i, 2550 ff. and 4810 ff.); another passage names 11 classes of Gandharvagandharvas (TĀr. i, 9, 3); the chief or leader of the Gandharvagandharvas is named Citra-rathacitrarathacitraratha (Bhag. x, 26); they are called the creatures of Prajāpatiprajāpati (Mn. i, 37) or of Brahmābrahmā (Hariv. 11793) or of Kaśyapakaśyapa (11850) or of the Munimunis (MBh. i, 2550; Hariv. 11553) or of Prādhāprādhā (MBh. i, 2556) or of Ariṣṭāariṣṭā (Hariv. 234; VP. i, 21) or of Vācvāc (PadmaP.); with Jainajainas the Gandharvagandharvas constitute one of the eight classes of the Vyantaravyantaras] [346,1]

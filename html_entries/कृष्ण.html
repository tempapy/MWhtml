Nandananda with his wife Yaśo-dāyaśodāyaśodā took the infant Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa and settled first in Gokulagokula or Vrajavraja, and afterwards in Vṛndāvanavṛndāvana, where Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa and Bala-rāmabalarāmabalarāma grew up together, roaming in the woods and joining in the sports of the herdsmen's sons ; Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa as a youth contested the sovereignty of Indraindra, and was victorious over that god, who descended from heaven to praise Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa, and made him lord over the cattle [Hariv. 3787 ff.; 7456 ff.; VP.]; Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa is described as sporting constantly with the Gopīgopīs or shepherdesses [Hariv. 4078 ff.; 8301 ff.; VP.; Gīt.] of whom a thousand became his wives, though only eight are specified, Rādhārādhā being the favourite [Hariv. 6694 ff.; 9177 ff.; VP.]; Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa built and fortified a city called Dvārakādvārakā in Gujarāt, and thither transported the inhabitants of Mathurāmathurā after killing Kaṉsakaṃsa; Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa had various wives besides the Gopīgopīs, and by Rukmiṇīrukmiṇī had a son Pradyumnapradyumna who is usually identified with Kāma-devakāmadevakāmadeva; with Jains, Kṛṣṇakṛṣṇa is one of the nine black Vasu-devavasudevavasudevas; with Buddhists he is the chief of the black demons, who are the enemies of Buddhabuddha and the white demons) [306,2]

• ṛṣiṛṣi
  • m. (√2. ṛṣṛṣ Comm. on Uṇ. iv, 119; ṛṣatiṛṣati jñānenajñānena saṃsāra-pāramsaṃsārapāramsaṃsārapāram, T.; perhaps fr. an obsolete √ṛṣṛṣ for √dṛśdṛś, ‘to see ?’ cf. ṛṣi-kṛtṛṣikṛtṛṣikṛt), a singer of sacred hymns, an inspired poet or sage, any person who alone or with others invokes the deities in rhythmical speech or song of a sacred character (e.g. the ancient hymn-singers Kutsakutsa, Atriatri, Rebharebha, Agastyaagastya, Kuśikakuśika, Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha, Vy-aśvavyaśvavyaśva), RV.; AV.; VS. &c. [226,3]
  • the Ṛṣiṛṣis were regarded by later generations as patriarchal sages or saints, occupying the same position in India history as the heroes and patriarchs of other countries, and constitute a peculiar class of beings in the early mythical system, as distinct from gods, men, Asuraasuras, &c., AV. x, 10, 26; ŚBr.; AitBr.; KātyŚr.; Mn. &c. [226,3]
  • they are the authors or rather seers of the Vedic hymns i.e. according to orthodox Hindū ideas they are the inspired personages to whom these hymns were revealed, and such an expression as ‘the Ṛṣiṛṣi says’ is equivalent to ‘so it stands in the sacred text’ [227,1]
  • seven Ṛṣiṛṣis, saptasapta ṛṣayaḥṛṣayaḥ, or saptaṛṣayaḥsaptaṛṣayaḥ or saptarṣayaḥsaptarṣayaḥ, are often mentioned in the Brāhmaṇabrāhmaṇas and later works as typical representatives of the character and spirit of the pre-historic or mythical period [227,1]
  • in ŚBr. xiv, 5, 2, 6 their names are given as follows, Gotamagotama, Bharadvājabharadvāja, Viśvā-mitraviśvāmitraviśvāmitra, Jamadagnijamadagni, Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha, Kaśyapakaśyapa, and Atriatri [227,1]
  • in MBh. xii, Marīcimarīci, Atriatri, Aṅgirasaṅgiras, Pulahapulaha, Kratukratu, Pulastyapulastya, Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha are given as the names of the Ṛṣiṛṣis of the first Manvantaramanvantara, and they are also called Prajāpatiprajāpatis or patriarchs [227,1]
  • the names of the Ṛṣiṛṣis of the subsequent Manv-antaramanvantaramanvantaras are enumerated in Hariv. 417 ff. [227,1]
  • afterwards three other names are added, viz. Pracetaspracetas or Dakṣadakṣa, Bhṛgubhṛgu, and Nāradanārada, these ten being created by Manumanu Svāyambhuvasvāyambhuva for the production of all other beings including gods and men, ĀśvŚr.; MBh.; VP. &c. [227,1]
  • in astron. the seven Ṛṣiṛṣis form the constellation of ‘the Great Bear’ RV. x, 82, 2; AV. vi, 40, 1; ŚBr.; ĀśvGṛ.; MBh. &c. [227,1]
  • (metaphorically the seven Ṛṣiṛṣis may stand for the seven senses or the seven vital airs of the body, VS. xxxiv; ŚBr. xiv; KātyŚr.) [227,1]
  • a saint or sanctified sage in general, an ascetic, anchorite (this is a later sense; sometimes three orders of these are enumerated, viz. Devarṣidevarṣis, Brahmarṣibrahmarṣis, and Rājarṣirājarṣis; sometimes seven, four others being added, viz. Maharṣimaharṣis, Paramarṣiparamarṣis, Śrutarṣiśrutarṣis, and Kāṇḍarṣikāṇḍarṣis), Mn. iv, 94 [227,1]
  • xi, 236; Śak.; Ragh. &c. [227,1]

  • the Vedaveda Comm. on MBh. and, Pat. [227,1]
  • a symbolical expression for the number seven [227,1]
  • the moon [227,1]

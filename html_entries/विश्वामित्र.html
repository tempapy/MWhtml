  • viśvā-mitraviśvāmitraviśvāmitra
  • m. (prob.) ‘friend of all’, N. of a celebrated Ṛṣiṛṣi or Sage (having the patr. Gāthinagāthina, Gādheyagādheya, and Jāhnavajāhnava; he was at first a functionary, together with Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha, of Su-dāssudāssudās, king of the Tṛtsutṛtsus; seeing Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha preferred by the king, he went over to the Bharatabharatas, but could not prevent their being defeated by Su-dāssudāssudās, although he caused the waters of the rivers Vipāśvipāś and Śutudrīśutudrī to retire and so give the Bharatabharatas free passage, RV. iii, 33; he was born as a Kṣatriyakṣatriya, deriving his lineage from an ancestor of Kuśikakuśika, named Purū-ravaspurūravaspurūravas, of the lunar race of kings, and himself sovereign of Kanyā-kubjakanyākubjakanyākubja or Kanoj; his fame rests chiefly on his contests with the great Brāhman Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha, and his success in elevating himself. though a Kṣatriyakṣatriya, to the rank of a Brāhman See Manu vii, 42 : the Rāmāyaṇarāmāyaṇa, which makes him a companion and counsellor of the young Rāma-candrarāmacandrarāmacandra, records [i, 51-65] how Viśvāmitraviśvāmitra, on his accession to the throne, visited Vasiṣṭhavasiṣṭha's hermitage, and seeing there the cow of plenty [probably typical of gogo, ‘the earth’], offered him untold treasures in exchange for it, but being refused, prepared to take it by force; a long contest ensued between the king and the saint [symbolical of the struggles between the Kṣatriyakṣatriya and Brāhmanical classes], which ended in the defeat of Viśvāmitraviśvāmitra, whose vexation was such that, in order to become a Brāhman and thus conquer his rival, he devoted himself to intense austerities [during which he was seduced by the nymph Menakāmenakā and had by her a daughter, Śakuntalāśakuntalā], gradually increasing the rigour of his mortification through thousands of years, till he successively earned the titles of Rājarṣirājarṣi, Ṛṣiṛṣi, Maharṣimaharṣi, and finally Brahmarṣibrahmarṣi ; he is supposed to be the author of nearly the whole of RV. iii, and of ix, 67, 1315; x, 137, 5; 167; moreover, a law-book, a Dhanurvedadhanurveda, and a medical wk. are attributed to him), RV. &c. &c.

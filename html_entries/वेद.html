  • N. of certain celebrated works which constitute the basis of the first period of the Hindū religion (these works were primarily three, viz. 1. the Ṛg-vedaṛgvedaṛgveda, 2. the Yajur-vedayajurvedayajurveda [of which there are, however, two divisions See taittirīya-saṃhitātaittirīyasaṃhitātaittirīyasaṃhitā, vājasaneyi-saṃhitāvājasaneyisaṃhitāvājasaneyisaṃhitā], 3. the Sāma-vedasāmavedasāmaveda ; these three works are sometimes called collectively trayītrayī, ‘the triple Vidyāvidyā’ or ‘threefold knowledge’, but the Ṛg-vedaṛgvedaṛgveda is really the only original work of the three, and much the most ancient [the oldest of its hymns being assigned by some who rely on certain astronomical calculations to a period between 4000 and 2500 B.C., before the settlement of the Āryans in India; and by others who adopt a different reckoning to a period between 1400 and 1000 B.C., when the Āryans had settled down in the Panjāb]; subsequently a fourth Vedaveda was added, called the Atharva-vedaatharvavedaatharvaveda, which was probably not completely accepted till after Manumanu, as his law-book often speaks of the three Vedavedas-calling them trayamtrayam brahmabrahma sanātanamsanātanam, ‘the triple eternal Vedaveda’, but only once [xi, 33] mentions the revelation made to Atharvanatharvan and Aṅgirasaṅgiras, without, however, calling it by the later name of Atharva-vedaatharvavedaatharvaveda; each of the four Vedavedas has two distinct parts, viz. 1. Mantramantra, i.e. words of prayer and adoration often addressed either to fire or to some form of the sun or to some form of the air, sky, wind &c., and praying for health, wealth, long life, cattle, offspring, victory, and even forgiveness of sins, and 2. Brāhmaṇabrāhmaṇa, consisting of Vidhividhi and Artha-vādaarthavādaarthavāda, i.e. directions for the detail of the ceremonies at which the Mantramantras were to be used and explanations of the legends &c. connected with the Mantramantras [see brāhmaṇabrāhmaṇa, vidhividhi], both these portions being termed śrutiśruti, revelation orally communicated by the Deity, and heard but not composed or written down by men [cf. IW. 24 &c.], although it is certain that both Mantramantras and Brāhmaṇabrāhmaṇas were compositions spread over a considerable period, much of the latter being comparatively modern; as the Vedavedas are properly three, so the Mantramantras are properly of three forms, 1. Ṛcṛc, which are verses of praise in metre, and intended for loud recitation; 2. Yajusyajus, which are in prose, and intended for recitation in a lower tone at sacrifices; 3. Sāmansāman, which are in metre, and intended for chanting at the Somasoma or Moon-plant ceremonies, the Mantramantras of the fourth or Atharva-vedaatharvavedaatharvaveda having no special name; but it must be borne in mind that the Yajuryajur and Sāma-vedasāmavedasāmaveda hymns, especially the latter, besides their own Mantramantras, borrow largely from the Ṛg-vedaṛgvedaṛgveda; the Yajur-vedayajurvedayajurveda and Sāma-vedasāmavedasāmaveda being in fact not so much collections of prayers and hymns as special prayer- and hymn-books intended as manuals for the Adhvaryuadhvaryu and Udgātṛudgātṛ priests respectively [see yajur-vedayajurvedayajurveda, sāma-vedasāmavedasāmaveda]; the Atharva-vedaatharvavedaatharvaveda, on the other hand, is, like the Ṛg-vedaṛgvedaṛgveda, a real collection of original hymns mixed up with incantations, borrowing little from the Ṛgṛg and having no direct relation to sacrifices, but supposed by mere recitation to produce long life, to cure diseases, to effect the ruin of enemies &c.; each of the four Vedavedas seems to have passed through numerous Śākhāśākhās or schools, giving rise to various recensions of the text, though the Ṛg-vedaṛgvedaṛgveda is only preserved in the Śākalaśākala recension, while a second recension, that of the Bhāṣkalabhāṣkalas, is only known by name; a tradition makes Vyāsavyāsa the compiler and arranger of the Vedavedas in their present form: they each have an Index or Anukramaṇīanukramaṇī [q.v.], the principal work of this kind being the general Index or Sarvânukramaṇīsarvānukramaṇī [q.v.]; out of the Brāhmaṇabrāhmaṇa portion of the Vedaveda grew two other departments of Vedic literature, sometimes included under the general name Vedaveda, viz. the strings of aphoristic rules, called Sūtrasūtras [q.v.], and the mystical treatises on the nature of God and the relation of soul and matter, called Upaniṣadupaniṣad [q.v.], which were appended to the Āraṇyakaāraṇyakas [q.v.], and became the real Vedaveda of thinking Hindūs, leading to the Darśanadarśanas or systems of philosophy; in the later literature the name of ‘fifth Vedaveda’ is accorded to the Itihāsaitihāsas or legendary epic poems and to the Purāṇapurāṇas, and certain secondary Vedavedas or Upa-vedaupavedaupavedas [q.v.] are enumerated; the Vedâṅgavedāṅgas or works serving as limbs [for preserving the integrity] of the Vedaveda are explained under vedā̑ṅgavedāṅga below: the only other works included under the head of Vedaveda being the Pariśiṣṭapariśiṣṭas, which supply rules for the ritual omitted in the Sūtrasūtras;

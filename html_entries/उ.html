• uu
  • the fifth letter and third short vowel of the alphabet, pronounced as the u in full. [171,3]
  • uu
  • ind. an interjection of compassion, anger, L. [171,3]
  • a particle implying assent, calling, command, L. [171,3]
  • uu
  • ind. an enclitic copula used frequently in the Vedavedas; [171,3]
  • (as a particle implying restriction and antithesis, generally after pronominals, prepositions, particles, and before nunu and susu, equivalent to) and, also, further; on the other hand (especially in connexion with a relative e.g. yaya uu, he on the contrary who &c.) [171,3]
  • This particle may serve to give emphasis, like idid and evaeva, especially after prepositions or demonstrative pronouns, in conjunction with nunu, vaivai, hihi, cidcid, &c. (e.g. ayamayam uu vāmvām purutamopurutamo... johavītijohavīti [RV. iii, 62, 2], this very person [your worshipper] invokes you &c.) It is especially used in the figure of speech called Anaphora, and particularly when the pronouns are repeated (e.g. tamtam uu stuṣastuṣa indramindram tamtam gṛṇīṣegṛṇīṣe [RV. ii, 20, 4], him I praise, Indraindra, him I sing). It may be used in drawing a conclusion, like the English ‘now’ (e.g. tadtad uu tathātathā nana kuryātkuryāt [ŚBr. v, 2, 2, 3], that now he should not do in such a manner), and is frequently found in interrogative sentences (e.g. kaka uu tactac ciketaciketa [RV. i, 164, 48], who, I ask, should know that?) [171,3]
  • Pāṇinipāṇini calls this particle uñuñ to distinguish it from the interrogative uu. In the Pada-pāṭhapadapāṭhapadapāṭha it is written ūmūm. [171,3]
  • In the classical language uu occurs only after athaatha, nana, and kimkim, with a slight modification of the sense, and often only as an expletive (See kimkim); [171,3]
  • uu — uu or uu — utauta, on the one hand - on the other hand; partly — partly; as well — as. [171,3]

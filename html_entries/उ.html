• u the fifth letter and third short vowel of the alphabet, pronounced as the u in full. [171,3]
  • u ind. an interjection of compassion, anger, L. [171,3]
  • a particle implying assent, calling, command, L. [171,3]
  • u ind. an enclitic copula used frequently in the Vedas; [171,3]
  • (as a particle implying restriction and antithesis, generally after pronominals, prepositions, particles, and before nu and su, equivalent to) and, also, further; on the other hand (especially in connexion with a relative e.g. ya u, he on the contrary who &c.) [171,3]
  • This particle may serve to give emphasis, like id and eva, especially after prepositions or demonstrative pronouns, in conjunction with nu, vai, hi, cid, &c. (e.g. ayam u vām purutamo... johavīti [RV. iii, 62, 2], this very person [your worshipper] invokes you &c.) It is especially used in the figure of speech called Anaphora, and particularly when the pronouns are repeated (e.g. tam u stuṣa indram tam gṛṇīṣe [RV. ii, 20, 4], him I praise, Indra, him I sing). It may be used in drawing a conclusion, like the English ‘now’ (e.g. tad u tathā na kuryāt [ŚBr. v, 2, 2, 3], that now he should not do in such a manner), and is frequently found in interrogative sentences (e.g. ka u tac ciketa [RV. i, 164, 48], who, I ask, should know that?) [171,3]
  • Pāṇini calls this particle uñ to distinguish it from the interrogative u. In the Pada-pāṭhapada-pāṭhapadapāṭha it is written ūm. [171,3]
  • In the classical language u occurs only after atha, na, and kim, with a slight modification of the sense, and often only as an expletive (See kim); [171,3]
  • u — u or u — uta, on the one hand - on the other hand; partly — partly; as well — as. [171,3]
  • u cl. 5. P. unoti (See vy-uvy-uvyu, RV. v, 31, 1) : cl. 2. Ā. (1. sg. uve, RV. x, 86, 7) : cl. 1. Ā. avate, Dhātup.;
    to call to, hail;
    to roar, bellow (See also ȏta = ā-utaā-utaāuta). [171,3]

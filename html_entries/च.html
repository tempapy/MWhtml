• caca
  • the 20th letter of the alphabet, 1st of the 2nd (or palatal) class of consonants, having the sound of ch in church. [380,1]
  • caca
  • ind. and, both, also, moreover, as well as (= τε, Lat. que, placed like these particles as an enclitic after the word which it connects with what precedes; when used with a personal pronoun this must appear in its fuller accented form (e.g. tavatava caca mamamama caca [not tete caca meme caca], ‘both of thee and me’), when used after verbs the first of them is accented, Pāṇ. viii, 1, 58 f.; it connects whole sentences as well as parts of sentences; in RV. the double caca occurs more frequently than the single (e.g. ahaṃahaṃ caca tvaṃtvaṃ caca, ‘I and thou’, viii, 62, 11); the double caca may also be used somewhat redundantly in class. Sanskṛt (e.g. kvakva hariṇakānāṃhariṇakānāṃ jīvitaṃjīvitaṃ cātilolaṃcātilolaṃ kvakva caca vajra-sārāḥvajrasārāḥvajrasārāḥ śarāsśarās tete, ‘where is the frail existence, of fawns and where are thy adamantine arrows?’ Śak. i, 10); in later literature, however, the first caca is more usually omitted (e.g. ahaṃahaṃ tvaṃtvaṃ caca), and when more than two things are enumerated only one caca is often found (e.g. tejasātejasā yaśasāyaśasā lakṣmyālakṣmyā sthityāsthityā caca parayāparayā, ‘in glory, in fame, in beauty, and in high position’ Nal. xii, 6); elsewhere, when more than two things are enumerated, caca is placed after some and omitted after others (e.g. ṛṇa-dātāṛṇadātāṛṇadātā caca vaidyaśvaidyaś caca śrotriyośrotriyo nadīnadī, ‘the payer of a debt and a physician [and] a Brāhman [and] a river’ Hit. i, 4, 55); in Ved. and even in class. Sanskṛt [Mn. iii, 20; ix, 322; Hit.], when the double caca would generally be used, the second may occasionally be omitted (e.g. indraśindraś caca somasoma, ‘both Indraindra [and thou] Somasoma’ RV. vii, 104, 25; durbhedyaśdurbhedyaś cāśusaṃdheyaḥcāśusaṃdheyaḥ, ‘both difficult to be divided [and] quickly united’ Hit. i); with lexicographers caca may imply a reference to certain other words which are not expressed (e.g. kamaṇḍalaukamaṇḍalau caca karakaḥkarakaḥ, ‘the word karakakaraka has the meaning ‘pitcher’ and other meanings’); sometimes caca is = evaeva, even, indeed, certainly, just (e.g. su-cintitaṃsucintitaṃsucintitaṃ cauṣadhaṃcauṣadhaṃ nana nāma-mātreṇanāmamātreṇanāmamātreṇa karotykaroty arogamarogam, ‘even a well-devised remedy does not cure a disease by its mere name’ Hit.; yāvantayāvanta evaeva tete tāvāṃścatāvāṃśca saḥsaḥ, ‘as great as they [were] just so great was he’ Ragh. xii, 45); occasionally caca is disjunctive, ‘but’, ‘on the contrary’, ‘on the other hand’, ‘yet’, ‘nevertheless’ (varamvaram ādyauādyau nana cāntimaḥcāntimaḥ, ‘better the two first but not the last’ Hit. ; śāntamśāntam idamidam āśrama-padaṃāśramapadaṃāśramapadaṃ sphuratisphurati caca bāhuḥbāhuḥ, ‘this hermitage is tranquil yet my arm throbs’ Śak. i, 15); ca-cacacacaca, though-yet, Vikr. ii, 9; ca-nacanacana caca, though - yet not, Pat.; caca - nana tutu (v.l. nanunanu) id., Mālav. iv, 8; nana caca - caca, though not - yet, Pat.; caca may be used for vāvā, ‘either’, ‘or’ (e.g. ihaiha cāmutracāmutra vāvā, ‘either here or hereafter’ Mn. xii, 89; strīstrī vāvā pumānpumān vāvā yacyac cānyatcānyat sattvamsattvam, ‘either a woman or a man or any other being’ R.), and when a neg. particle is joined with caca the two may then be translated by ‘neither’, ‘nor’; occasionally one caca or one nana is omitted (e.g. nana caca paribhoktuṃparibhoktuṃ naivanaiva śaknomiśaknomi hātumhātum, ‘I am able neither to enjoy nor to abandon’ Śak. v, 18; nana pūrvāhṇepūrvāhṇe nana caca parāhṇeparāhṇe, ‘neither in the forenoon nor in the afternoon’); ca-cacacacaca may express immediate connection between two acts or their simultaneous occurrence (e.g. mamamama caca muktaṃmuktaṃ tamasātamasā manomano manasijenamanasijena dhanuṣidhanuṣi śaraśśaraś caca niveśitaḥniveśitaḥ, ‘no sooner is my mind freed from darkness than a shaft is fixed on his bow by the heart-born god’, vi, 8); caca is sometimes = cedced, ‘if’ (cf. Pāṇ. viii, 1, 30; the verb is accented), RV.; AV.; MBh.; Vikr. ii, 20; Bhartṛ. ii, 45; caca may be used as an expletive (e.g. anyaiśanyaiś caca kratubhiśkratubhiś caca, ‘and with other sacrifices’); caca is often joined to an adv. like evaeva, apiapi, tathātathā, tathaivatathaiva, &c., either with or without a neg. particle (e.g. vairiṇaṃvairiṇaṃ nopasevetanopaseveta sahāyaṃsahāyaṃ caivacaiva vairiṇaḥvairiṇaḥ, ‘one ought not to serve either an enemy or the ally of an enemy’ Mn. iv, 133); (See evaeva, apiapi, &c.) For the meaning of caca after an interrogative See 2. kaka, 2. kathākathā, kimkim, kvakva); [380,1]

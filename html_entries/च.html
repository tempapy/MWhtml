• 1. caca
    • the 20th letter of the alphabet, 1st of the 2nd (or palatal) class of consonants, having the sound of ch in church. [380,1]
  • 2. caca ind.
    • and, both, also, moreover, as well as (= τε, Lat. que, placed like these particles as an enclitic after the word which it connects with what precedes; when used with a personal pronoun this must appear in its fuller accented form (e.g. tava caca mamamama caca [not tetatāti caca mememamāmi caca], ‘both of thee and me’), when used after verbs the first of them is accented, Pāṇ. viii, 1, 58 f.; it connects whole sentences as well as parts of sentences; in RV. the double caca occurs more frequently than the single (e.g. ahaṃahaṃahamahaah caca tvaṃtvaṃ caca, ‘I and thou’, viii, 62, 11); the double caca may also be used somewhat redundantly in class. Sanskṛt (e.g. kvakva hariṇakānāṃ jīvitaṃjīvita cā̑tilolaṃca kvakva caca vajra-sārāḥvajra śarāsśaraśaras tetatāti, ‘where is the frail existence, of fawns and where are thy adamantine arrows?’, Śak. i, 10); in later literature, however, the first caca is more usually omitted (e.g. ahaṃahaṃahamahaah tvaṃtvaṃtva caca), and when more than two things are enumerated only one caca is often found (e.g. tejasātejasatejas yaśasāyaśasayaśas lakṣmyālakṣmilakṣmī sthityāsthiti caca parayāparāpara, ‘in glory, in fame, in beauty, and in high position’, Nal. xii, 6); elsewhere, when more than two things are enumerated, caca is placed after some and omitted after others (e.g. ṛṇa-dātāṛṇadāta caca vaidyaś caca śrotriyo nadīnadīnadinad, ‘the payer of a debt and a physician [and] a Brāhman [and] a river’, Hit. i, 4, 55); in Ved. and even in class. Sanskṛt [Mn. iii, 20; ix, 322; Hit.], when the double caca would generally be used, the second may occasionally be omitted (e.g. indraśindra caca somasoma, ‘both Indra [and thou] Soma’, RV. vii, 104, 25; durbhedyaśdurbhedya cā̑śusaṃdheyaḥcaāśusaṃdheya, ‘both difficult to be divided [and] quickly united’, Hit. i); with lexicographers caca may imply a reference to certain other words which are not expressed (e.g. kamaṇḍalaukamaṇḍalu caca karakaḥ, ‘the word karakakaraka has the meaning ‘pitcher’ and other meanings’); sometimes caca is = evaeva, even, indeed, certainly, just (e.g. su-cintitaṃsucintitasu ca͡uṣadhaṃcaauṣadha nana nāma-mātreṇanāmamātreṇanāmamātranāmamātra karoty arogamaroga, ‘even a well-devised remedy does not cure a disease by its mere name’, Hit.; yāvantayāvat evaeva tetatāti tāvāṃśca saḥ, ‘as great as they [were] just so great was he’, Ragh. xii, 45); occasionally caca is disjunctive, ‘but’, ‘on the contrary’, ‘on the other hand’, ‘yet’, ‘nevertheless’ (varamvaramvara ādyauādyaādī nana cā̑ntimaḥcaantima, ‘better the two first but not the last’, Hit. ; śāntamśānta idamidamid āśrama-padaṃāśramapadaāśrama sphuratisphurat caca bāhuḥ, ‘this hermitage is tranquil yet my arm throbs’, Śak. i, 15); ca-cacaca, though-yet, Vikr. ii, 9; ca-nacanacana caca, though - yet not, Pat.; caca - nana tutu (v.l. nanunanu) id., Mālav. iv, 8; nana caca - caca, though not - yet, Pat.; caca may be used for vāvāvauū, ‘either’, ‘or’ (e.g. ihaiha cā̑mutracaamutra vāvāvauū, ‘either here or hereafter’, Mn. xii, 89; strīstrīstri vāvāvauū pumān vāvāvauūvāsvas yacyacyat cā̑nyatca sattvamsattva, ‘either a woman or a man or any other being’, R.), and when a neg. particle is joined with caca the two may then be translated by ‘neither’, ‘nor’; occasionally one caca or one nana is omitted (e.g. nana caca paribhoktuṃ na͡ivanaivananāeva śaknomi hātumhātu, ‘I am able neither to enjoy nor to abandon’, Śak. v, 18; nana pūrvā̑hṇepūrvāhṇapūrvapūrvā nana caca parā̑hṇeparāhṇaparaparā, ‘neither in the forenoon nor in the afternoon’); ca-cacaca may express immediate connection between two acts or their simultaneous occurrence (e.g. mamamama caca muktaṃ tamasātamasātamasatamas manomanomanumanasmana manasijenamanasija dhanuṣidhanuṣ śaraś caca niveśitaḥ, ‘no sooner is my mind freed from darkness than a shaft is fixed on his bow by the heart-born god’, vi, 8); caca is sometimes = cȇdcedcaid, ‘if’ (cf. Pāṇ. viii, 1, 30; the verb is accented), RV.; AV.; MBh.; Vikr. ii, 20; Bhartṛ. ii, 45; caca may be used as an expletive (e.g. anyaiśanya caca kratubhiś caca, ‘and with other sacrifices’); caca is often joined to an adv. like evaeva, apiapiapīap, tathātathā, tatha͡ivatathāeva, &c., either with or without a neg. particle (e.g. vairiṇaṃvairiṇavairin nȏpasevetananā sahā̑yaṃsahasahāayamayaāyamāya caiva vairiṇaḥ, ‘one ought not to serve either an enemy or the ally of an enemy’, Mn. iv, 133); (See evaeva, apiapiapīap, &c.) For the meaning of caca after an interrogative See 2. kaka, 2. kathākathākath, kimkimkikiṃ, kvakva); [380,1]

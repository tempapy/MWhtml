SYLLOGISM [793-a], s. nyAyaH, avayavI m. (n), avayavaGawitavAkyaM, avayavayukta- vAkyaM, avayavopetavAkyaM, AnupUrvvaM. A syllogism has five mem- bers or avayavAH, according to the Nyāya system, viz. 1. The proposition or assertion to be proved, pratijYA; 2. the reason given, hetuH; 3. the example or illustration, udAharaRaM; 4. the application, upanayaH; 5. the conclusion, nigamanaM. Thus, 1. ‘The hill is fiery,’ parvvato vahnimAn. 2. ‘Because it smokes,’ DUmavattvAt. 3. ‘Whatever smokes is fiery, like a culinary hearth,’ yo yo DUmavAn sa vahnimAn yaTA mahAnasaH. 4. ‘This (hill) has smoke which is always accompanied with fire,’ vahni- vyApyaDUmavAn ayaM. 5. ‘Therefore it is fiery,’ tasmAd vahnimAn. According to the Mīmānsā school, a syllogism has only the last three members.
